Research

Data Programming for Alarm Suppression System Evaluation

False alarms generated by physiological monitors can overwhelm clinical caretakers with a variety of alarms. The resulting alarm fatigue can be mitigated with alarm suppression. Before being deployed, such suppression mechanisms need to be evaluated through a costly observational study, which would determine and label the truly suppressible alarms. We propose a lightweight method for evaluating alarm suppression without access to the true alarm labels. The method is based on the data programming paradigm, which combines noisy and cheap-to-obtain labeling heuristics into probabilistic labels. Based on these labels, the method estimates the sensitivity/specificity of a suppression mechanism and describes the likely outcomes of an observational study in the form of confidence bounds.

Smart Alarms 2.0

Many devices used to continuously monitor vital signs in hospitals and home settings generate alarms to indicate changes in patient status to (both in-hospital and at home) caregivers. However, less than 1% of these alarms are considered actionable or informative, and consequently cause clinical alarm fatigue. Smart Alarms 2.0 will utilize caregiver-in-the-loop alarm suppression systems to reduce the frequency of non-informative alarms in-clinic and at home.
